Dynamic Stretching
What Is It? Uses controlled, active movement through a full range of motion. No holding at the end range—movement is continuous and rhythmic. Performed before training as a warm-up to prepare the body for performance.

Why It Works:
  • Increases blood flow and muscle temperature; warm muscles contract more powerfully and are less injury-prone.
  • Activates the nervous system so the body is primed neurologically for fast, coordinated movement.
  • Improves joint lubrication by distributing synovial fluid across joint surfaces.
  • Rehearses movement patterns specifically mimicking your training.

Key Principles: Controlled momentum (not passive flopping, not max force), gradual range increase (start at 50% and build), quality over speed, and absolutely no holding (otherwise it becomes static stretching).
Static Stretching
What Is It? Sustained holding of a stretch at its end range. Holds under 15 seconds are largely ineffective because the stretch reflex is still active. Holding for 15-20+ seconds allows the nervous system to stop resisting so the muscle can relax into the stretch. Perform after training when muscles are warm and pliable.

The Stretch Reflex: When a muscle is lengthened too far or too fast, muscle spindles fire a signal causing immediate muscle contraction to protect it from tearing. Bouncing is strictly forbidden as it triggers this reflex.

PNF Stretching: Proprioceptive Neuromuscular Facilitation is more effective for range of motion by exploiting:
  • Autogenic Inhibition: Isometric contraction fires the Golgi Tendon Organ, inhibiting muscle spindles to allow deeper relaxation upon release.
  • Reciprocal Inhibition: Contracting an opposing muscle automatically relaxes the target muscle.
PNF Protocol: Stretch to end range (10s) → Isometric contraction of stretched muscle (10s) → Relax and move deeper (10s).
Plyometrics
What Is It? Utilizing the Stretch-Shortening Cycle (SSC)—a rapid sequence of eccentric loading (lengthening) followed immediately by concentric contraction (shortening). Shorter ground contact time maximizes elastic energy utility and explosive power output.

SSC Phases:
  • Eccentric Phase: Muscle lengthens and stores elastic energy like a spring.
  • Concentric Phase: Stored energy is released explosively.

Adaptations:
  • Neural: Increased motor unit recruitment, improved Rate of Force Development (RFD), and better intermuscular coordination.
  • Structural: Increased tendon stiffness (storing energy more efficiently) and fast-twitch muscle fiber type shift.

Tips: Never train plyos after heavy leg strength work in the same session; train before when the nervous system is fresh. Establish a safe landing mechanic before advancing intensity.
Weight Training & Hypertrophy
3 Mechanisms of Muscle Growth: Mechanical Tension (loading through range of motion under resistance), Metabolic Stress (byproduct buildup from repeated contractions), and Muscle Damage (micro-tears from eccentric loading repaired larger).

Equipment Options:
  • Barbells: Best for maximum loading, synchronous bilateral work, and heavy compound stability. Ideal rep range: 3-8 reps.
  • Dumbbells: Best for greater range of motion, fixing muscle imbalances, and high unilateral stabilizer demand. Ideal rep range: 8-15 reps.
  • Machines: Best for targeted isolation, pure muscle fatigue without stabilizer demands, and technical breakdown management. Ideal rep range: 10-20 reps.

What Are Stabilizers?: Stabilizer muscles work isometrically to hold positions and resist unwanted movement. They do not grow proportionally compared to primary movers because they lack equivalent mechanical tension or metabolic stress. Instead, they gain neural efficiency, tendon/connective tissue resilience, and structural endurance.

Compounds vs Isolation: Compounds recruit multiple muscle groups/joints for heavy loading and better athletic carryover. Isolation targets single muscles to finish work or address imbalances. Always prioritize compounds at the start of a session when energy is highest.
